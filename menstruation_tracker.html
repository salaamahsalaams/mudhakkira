Background

These rulings are not only for women — men should study them too. A husband may need to guide his wife, a father his daughters, a brother his sisters. Some rulings also directly affect men, such as the prohibition on issuing a divorce during menstruation.

This is the final chapter of the Fiqh of Ṭahārah (purification). Mastering it gives you clarity in real-life situations — knowing when worship is valid, what adjustments are required, and how the sharī'ah accommodates the natural cycle with mercy.

What Counts as Menstrual Blood

ConditionRule
Any colour counts Blood can be red, dark red, yellowish, or brownish — even a tiny amount counts as menstrual blood if it exits naturally.
Must exit naturally The blood must come by itself — not from injury or medication. Medically induced bleeding is not considered ḥayḍ.
Age limits apply Does not apply below roughly age 9 or after menopause (around age 50). Bleeding outside these bounds is not ḥayḍ.

Divorce & Menstruation

PointDetail
Ḥarām to divorce during period Issuing a divorce while a woman is menstruating is sinful. A judge will compel the man to take her back — the divorce still counts, but he has committed a sin.
Why it causes hardship If divorced during her period, that cycle does not count toward the three-cycle waiting period — making the wait longer and placing undue burden on her.
Counting cycles for ʿiddah For a cycle to count in the three-cycle waiting period, the woman must bleed for at least half a day. A tiny amount alone is not sufficient for counting.

General Rules

RuleDetail
Maximum: 15 days A period can never exceed 15 days. Any bleeding from day 16 onward is ignored — the woman is considered pure and resumes prayer and fasting normally.
Minimum purity gap: 15 days After the period ends, at least 15 full days of purity must pass before new blood counts as the start of a new cycle.
Sign 1 of purity — Al-Quṣṣah A white discharge with absolutely no trace of blood — this confirms the period has ended and she may perform ghusl and return to worship.
Sign 2 of purity — Al-Jafāf Complete dryness — a white cloth placed produces zero colour. Either sign is sufficient to confirm she is now pure.

Three Categories of Women

CategoryRule
Mubtada'ah — First Period A woman experiencing her very first period. The 15-day maximum applies completely — if she is still bleeding on day 16, she is considered pure and resumes worship even if blood continues.
Muʿtādah — Regular Cycle A woman who knows her usual cycle length. She gets 3 extra days beyond her usual before being considered pure — as long as the total does not exceed 15 days. Example: usual cycle = 7 days → she may bleed up to 10 days total.
Ḥāmil — Pregnant Woman A pregnant woman who bleeds follows the same ḥayḍ rulings with one difference: a different maximum depending on her trimester (see note below). Everything else is identical.

Pregnant women — maximum days by trimester: 0–3 months: 15 days  ·  3–6 months: 20 days  ·  6 months to birth: 30 days.

Common Anomalies

AnomalyRuling
Variable cycle length If every month is different, use the length of the last cycle as the baseline. Add 3 days — that is the limit before she is considered pure again.
Forgotten cycle length If she cannot remember, she estimates a range (e.g. 5–7 days). She takes the higher estimate as her baseline and adds 3 days (total: 10 days). From day 11 onward she is pure even if bleeding continues.
Interrupted bleeding If the period stops and restarts, count only the days of actual bleeding — pause the count on dry days and resume when bleeding returns. Once the total bleeding days reach her usual + 3, she is pure, even if blood continues afterward.

Postnatal Bleeding — Nifās

Nifās is the bleeding that occurs after childbirth. All the same rules of ḥayḍ apply — with one difference: the maximum duration is 60 days (not 15). If bleeding stops before 60 days, she is immediately pure. If still bleeding on day 61, she is considered pure regardless and resumes prayer. Once 15 full days of purity have passed after nifās ends, any new blood is considered a regular cycle.

What You Cannot Do During Menstruation

ProhibitionNotes
1. Prayer & Fasting Cannot pray or fast. Missed prayers during menstruation do not need to be made up — this is a mercy from Allah. Missed obligatory fasts (e.g. Ramadan) must be made up later.
2. Ṭawāf Cannot perform Ṭawāf around the Kaʿbah, as it is a form of prayer. If planning Ḥajj or ʿUmrah, contraception may be taken in advance to delay the period — this is permitted.
3. Entering the Masjid The vast majority of scholars agree she cannot enter the main prayer area. A minority opinion permits entering Western mosques when there is a genuine need (attending a class, volunteering), where the mosque functions as an Islamic community centre — though avoiding it is better if possible.
4. Touching the Qur'ān Cannot touch a complete Arabic Qur'ān, even with a barrier. A Qur'ān with translation, or one missing even one āyah, may be handled freely. Reading from a phone or tablet is permitted. Reciting from memory is permitted for menstruating women (unlike those in a state of janābah).
5. Sexual Intercourse Completely forbidden during menstruation.
6. Foreplay (private area) Foreplay involving the private area without a barrier between them is not permitted. With clothing as a barrier, it is permitted.

Prayer Rules When Becoming Pure Near Prayer Time

SituationWhat to Do
Which prayers to perform When she becomes pure during a prayer's time, she prays that prayer — and its combined partner if applicable (Ẓuhr with ʿAṣr, Maghrib with ʿIshā).
When time is very short The sharī'ah accounts for three things: (1) time to find water or soil for purification, (2) time to perform wuḍū or tayammum, and (3) time to catch at least 2 rakʿahs before the prayer time ends. If she has enough time for all three, she must pray.
If truly insufficient time If she genuinely cannot purify and catch 2 rakʿahs — even with tayammum — she does not need to make up that prayer at all.
While outside or shopping She must set off immediately to find water or ground for tayammum. Time spent on other tasks (finishing shopping, running errands) is not counted by the sharī'ah — if she delays and misses the prayer, she becomes responsible for making it up.

Takeaway

These rulings are a mercy — not a burden. Missed prayers are entirely forgiven, obligatory fasts are simply postponed, and every edge case has a clear ruling. Understanding them removes unnecessary confusion and replaces anxiety with clarity.
