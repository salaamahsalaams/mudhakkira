Ep 01

The Roadmap to Reading Qur'an with Understanding

Watch

Topic

7-Step Learning Roadmap — from Arabic literacy to Qur'ānic comprehension

Theme

Understanding the Qur'ān is achievable — and more accessible than most people think

Background

A common misconception: understanding Qur'anic Arabic means mastering the full Arabic language — which can take years. This is not correct. Qur'anic Arabic is a distinct goal with a clearly defined path. The language of the Qur'ān is so sublime that it froze the version of Arabic it was revealed in (Modern Standard Arabic), yet its most frequent vocabulary is remarkably accessible.

There are 7 steps from absolute scratch, or just 3 steps if you can already recite the Qur'ān. With consistent effort and the right intention, reaching your first khatmah with understanding is a realistic goal.

The 7 Steps

StepWhatDetails
1 Arabic Literacy Learn the Arabic alphabet and how letters connect to form words. Can be completed in a single focused day.
2 Long & Short Vowels Understand long vowels (ā, ū, ī) and short vowel signs — fatḥah, ḍammah, kasrah — the tashkeel that shapes pronunciation.
3 Special Sounds Master the Arabic sounds that don't exist in English — khā' (خ), ḍād (ض), ḥā' (ح), rā' (ر). Each has its own articulation technique.
4 Tajweed Rules Learn to recite the Qur'ān exactly as it was revealed — madd, idghām, ikhfā', and the other rules that protect the integrity of every word.
5 Common Vocabulary Work through frequency lists — the most repeated words in the Qur'ān. No need to learn words for everyday objects. Every word on this list appears hundreds of times across the text.
6 Core Grammar Study the most common Qur'ānic grammar structures using Introduction to Qur'anic Grammar. This transforms vocabulary into full comprehension — how much you need will vary by person.
7 Integration Apply everything learned — work through āyahs word by word, connecting vocabulary and grammar until the meaning of the Qur'ān opens up to you directly.

Already able to recite? Skip to Steps 5, 6, and 7 — you already have the foundation.

Key Points

PointDetail
Slow & steady beats rushing Going too deep too fast can backfire. Consistent weekly sessions — even short ones — outperform intense bursts that lead to burnout.
Right intention above all Do this only for the sake of Allah. A pure niyyah makes the path easier and the results come faster — by His permission.
You haven't fully read it yet You haven't truly read the Qur'ān until you've read it in Arabic with understanding. Not a criticism — a motivation. The door is open to everyone.

Takeaway

Understanding the Qur'ān is not a gift reserved for scholars — it is a goal available to everyone who pursues it with sincerity and structure. Start with the first step, and trust that the path is designed to be walked.

List 1 — Demonstratives & Relative Pronouns

Arabic Transliteration Meaning Notes
هَذَا hādhā This (masculine) Can be prefixed with prepositions — its shape changes slightly but remains recognisable in context.
هَذِهِ hādhihi This (feminine) / These Serves double duty — singular feminine "this" and plural "these." Less common in the Qur'ān, so don't overweight it at the start.
تِلْكَ tilka That (feminine) / Those Also double duty — "that" (feminine singular) and "those" (plural). Very frequent in Qur'ānic verses. e.g. تِلْكَ حُدُودُ اللَّهِ.
هَؤُلَاءِ hā'ulā'i These Contains two madd (elongations). The first (munfaṣil) may be shortened; the second (mutaṣil) must be extended 4–5 counts.
أُولَٰئِكَ ulā'ika Those Every occurrence in the Qur'ān contains madd mutaṣil — always extend 4–5 counts. e.g. أُولَٰئِكَ عَلَى هُدًى مِّن رَّبِّهِمْ.
الَّذِي alladhī He who / Which (masc.) Masculine singular relative pronoun. Often attaches to preceding words: كَالَّذِي (like the one who), بِالَّذِي (with the one who).
الَّتِي allatī She who / Which (fem.) Feminine counterpart to الَّذِي. Used with feminine singular nouns. e.g. النَّارَ الَّتِي أُعِدَّتْ لِلْكَافِرِينَ.
الَّذِينَ alladhīna Those who (plural) Used with plural nouns — masculine, feminine, or mixed. Appears in Al-Fātiḥah itself: صِرَاطَ الَّذِينَ أَنْعَمْتَ عَلَيْهِمْ.

How to Actually Learn These

MethodDetail
Use Memrise A Memrise list is linked in the video description. Drill and test yourself using the app — spaced repetition makes memorisation far more durable than re-reading.
Find them in your Muṣḥaf After learning the list, open your own copy of the Qur'ān and locate these words on the page. This bridges the gap between "learned" and "truly internalised."
Don't just park the list Learning vocabulary and then not using it leads to fast forgetting. After each session, find the words in actual āyahs — you will retain them far longer when you see them in context.

Takeaway

These 8 words are the first of a list of 72 that alone cover 45% of the Qur'ān. They are not obscure — they appear on almost every single page. Learn them, find them in your Muṣḥaf, and that is how vocabulary becomes fluency.
